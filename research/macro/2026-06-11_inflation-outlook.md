**1. Proximate Drivers**

Core PCE inflation reached an annual rate of 3.3% in April 2026, matching expectations and remaining well above the Fed’s 2% target [6]. Forecasts for 2026 headline PCE have been revised upward to 3.1%, with core PCE now seen at 2.8%, driven primarily by a sustained oil-price spike linked to the Iran conflict and associated energy-market disruptions [39]. March 2026 FOMC participants raised their median 2026 core PCE projection to 2.7%, 0.1 pp higher than the September 2025 median, reflecting incoming data that showed slower disinflation than previously anticipated [34].

Supply-side pressures have intensified in the near term. Global supply-chain indices remain elevated, and new tariffs have begun to raise import prices, contributing to goods inflation that had previously been cooling [11][12]. Professional forecasters now expect real GDP growth of only 2.2% for 2026 (down 0.3 pp from the prior survey), consistent with higher energy costs squeezing household real income and business margins [37].

**2. Structural Factors**

Persistent global supply shocks continue to dominate inflation dynamics. Research decomposing inflation trends attributes most of the slow-moving component of U.S. and foreign CPI to supply rather than demand disturbances, with correlated pandemic-era and geopolitical shocks propagating through trade networks [41]. Tariffs and trade-cost increases add a durable layer of upward pressure on goods prices, shifting relative prices in a manner that monetary policy cannot fully offset without inducing output losses [45][42].

Labor-market and expectations channels have also evolved. Unemployment is projected to average 4.4–4.5% through 2027, a level that no longer exerts strong downward pressure on wages [37]. At the same time, institutional flows into TIPS have influenced breakeven measures, though the effect is largest only during high-volatility episodes and explains only a modest share of observed movements [28]. These factors together suggest that the Phillips curve remains relatively flat while supply-curve shifts are more frequent.

**3. Policy Response**

The FOMC’s March 2026 Summary of Economic Projections shows participants expecting only gradual convergence of core PCE to 2.0% by 2028, with the median federal-funds-rate path revised higher than in December 2025 [34][30]. The Committee has signaled that an oil-driven inflation impulse reduces the likelihood of near-term rate cuts [23]. Professional forecasters similarly embed a higher policy-rate trajectory, with the unemployment rate remaining near 4.4% and growth subdued.

Outside the United States, the ECB kept policy rates unchanged in March 2026 and highlighted a “significantly more uncertain” outlook, citing both energy and trade risks [25]. Fiscal authorities face parallel constraints: higher debt-service costs and tariff revenues interact with elevated inflation, limiting room for additional stimulus [4]. Regulatory and trade-policy measures (tariffs, supply-chain diversification mandates) are expected to remain in place, reinforcing the supply-side inflation channel through 2027.

**4. Asset Transmission**

Higher and more persistent inflation has lifted market pricing of near-term policy rates, pushing 10-year Treasury yields and mortgage rates higher even as long-term inflation expectations remain anchored [18][19]. TIPS breakevens have widened modestly, though part of the movement reflects liquidity and flow effects rather than pure expectation shifts [28]. Equities face headwinds from compressed valuations in rate-sensitive sectors and from margin pressure in energy-intensive industries, while commodity prices—especially crude oil—have risen sharply on geopolitical supply concerns [23][39].

The dollar has been supported by relatively hawkish Fed repricing versus other major central banks, widening interest-rate differentials. Credit spreads have remained contained so far, but any further upside surprises in core services inflation could prompt a repricing of risk assets and a steeper yield curve.

**5. Outlook**

The baseline trajectory is one of slow disinflation: headline PCE is projected to average 3.1% in 2026 before declining toward 2.3–2.5% in 2027, with core measures remaining above 2.5% through year-end 2026 [39][34]. The Fed is expected to hold the policy rate steady longer than markets had priced in late 2025, producing a higher-for-longer path that supports a modest further rise in the unemployment rate to 4.5%. Growth is forecast at 2.2% for 2026, sufficient to avoid recession but insufficient to generate rapid slack.

Key downside risks include an escalation of Middle East conflict that sustains oil above $100/bbl, pushing 2026 PCE above 3.5% and forcing additional monetary tightening [23][39]. Upside risks to inflation would also arise from broader tariff implementation or renewed supply-chain bottlenecks. Conversely, a sharper growth slowdown could bring inflation back to target faster, reopening the door to earlier rate cuts.
