### C. Fed Policy & Inflation

As of late June 2026, the Effective Fed Funds Rate sits at 3.63%, while both the 5-year and 10-year breakeven inflation rates are hovering near 2.24% and 2.22%, respectively. This implies a real fed funds rate of approximately 1.4%, a level that historically has not been sufficient to quell inflation when the economy is generating price increases of the magnitude we are currently seeing. The most recent CPI and PCE readings, at 4.17% and 4.07% year-over-year respectively as of May, are running nearly double the breakeven rates, indicating that market participants are pricing in a relatively rapid disinflation that the hard data has yet to confirm. Compounding this concern is the unemployment rate, which at 4.30% remains historically low and suggests the labor market is still operating with considerable slack absorbed, keeping upward pressure on wages and services inflation. The inflation-jobs balance is therefore skewed decidedly toward overheating; the Fed is not seeing the cooling in the labor market that would typically accompany a successful inflation fight, which reduces the urgency for rate cuts and increases the risk that the current policy stance is insufficient.

The Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index, currently reading -0.516, adds another layer of complexity. A negative value indicates financial conditions that are looser than average, which is the opposite of what the Fed needs to achieve its inflation goals. When financial conditions are this accommodative—driven by buoyant equity markets, narrow credit spreads, and ample liquidity—monetary policy is effectively being undermined. The transmission mechanism of higher rates is being blunted by the market’s willingness to lend and borrow on favorable terms, meaning the Fed’s 3.63% funds rate is not translating into the restraint required to bring CPI and PCE back toward 2%. This disconnect between the policy rate and actual financial conditions is a critical signal that the current level of rates is not restrictive enough.

Turning to recent Fed communications, the official press releases and speeches provide little comfort for those expecting near-term easing. Governor Cook’s welcome remarks on June 24 offered no policy guidance, while the subsequent enforcement actions and stress test results—confirming that large banks can weather a severe recession—implicitly signal that the Fed sees the financial system as resilient enough to handle higher rates without systemic risk. This resilience removes a key argument for cutting rates preemptively. Taken together, the data and commentary strongly suggest that the probability of a rate cut in the near term is negligible, and the market should instead be pricing in a material risk of a rate hike. The most forward-looking signal, however, comes from copper, which slipped 0.3% to $37.23.
