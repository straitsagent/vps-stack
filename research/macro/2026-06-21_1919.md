### E. Commodities

The current snapshot of commodity markets presents a fascinating tableau of stasis that belies the intense crosscurrents beneath the surface. Meanwhile, Brent crude at $80.6 is locked in a delicate equilibrium, reflecting a market that is simultaneously pricing in tepid global growth expectations and a geopolitical risk premium that refuses to dissipate. The flat price indicates that traders see neither a supply shock nor a demand collapse on the immediate horizon, yet the level itself—well above pre-conflict norms—implies that the market is bracing for potential disruptions in key transit chokepoints or a sudden shift in OPEC+ discipline. Copper’s stagnation at $6,337 is perhaps the most telling signal for the global industrial cycle. As a leading indicator, copper’s inability to rally despite ongoing narratives of green energy transition and electrification suggests that real-time demand from China’s property sector and global manufacturing is underwhelming. The metal is effectively flashing amber for industrial output, indicating that the anticipated cyclical recovery may be delayed or shallower than hoped. Finally, natural gas at $3.198 provides critical energy cost context: this level, while not crisis-driven, is high enough to weigh on European and Asian industrial competitiveness, particularly as winter storage withdrawals begin. It reinforces that energy costs remain a structural headwind for manufacturing margins, even as headline inflation moderates. Collectively, these flat prices are not a sign of calm but of a market caught between competing narratives—inflation persistence versus growth deceleration, geopolitical risk versus demand weakness—with each commodity acting as a distinct lens on a fragmented global economy.
