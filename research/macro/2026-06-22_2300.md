### C. Fed Policy & Inflation

As of mid-June 2026, the effective federal funds rate sits at 3.63%, a level that is notably accommodative when measured against the most recent headline inflation prints. The year-over-year Consumer Price Index stands at 4.17% and the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index, the Fed’s preferred gauge, is at 3.77%, meaning that the real policy rate is deeply negative. This is not a neutral or restrictive posture; it is a stimulative one. The market’s own inflation expectations, as captured by breakeven rates, are telling a story of stubborn persistence rather than a return to target. The 5-year breakeven is at 2.27% and the 10-year breakeven at 2.25%, both hovering just above the Fed’s 2% objective. This suggests that bond market participants do not believe current policy settings are sufficient to bring inflation down sustainably, and they are pricing in a long-term equilibrium that is slightly above the Fed’s comfort zone. The unemployment rate at 4.30% provides the critical context for the Fed’s hesitation. While this is a low level by historical standards, it represents a notable softening from the sub-4% readings of recent years, and it signals that the labor market is no longer overheating. The balance between inflation and jobs has shifted: inflation remains too high, but the labor market is showing clear signs of cooling, creating a dilemma for policymakers who fear that aggressive tightening could tip the economy into a recession while insufficient action allows inflation to become entrenched.

### E. Commodities

The current cross-commodity landscape presents a nuanced picture of an economy caught between persistent inflationary pressures and decelerating growth momentum. Gold’s marginal decline to $4,220, despite its traditional role as a risk-off and inflation hedge, suggests that the market is not currently pricing in acute systemic stress or a runaway price spiral; instead, the metal’s stability reflects a wait-and-see posture, with investors reluctant to chase safe havens in the absence of a clear catalyst, such as a sudden credit event or a sharp dovish pivot from central banks. In stark contrast, Brent crude’s 2.0% drop to $78.2 signals that the growth and geopolitical premium is rapidly deflating, likely driven by easing supply disruption fears—perhaps from a de-escalation in key producing regions—and a growing consensus that global industrial demand is softening, which undermines the narrative of tight physical markets. This bearish sentiment in oil is corroborated by copper’s flat performance at $6.377, a critical leading indicator that is essentially treading water; the red metal’s inability to rally, even amid hopes for Chinese stimulus or a green energy transition, implies that the global factory floor is experiencing a genuine lull, with order books and manufacturing PMIs failing to provide the necessary spark for a reflation trade. Meanwhile, natural gas’s 1.6% advance to $3.284 offers a counterpoint, reflecting localized energy cost dynamics—likely tied to seasonal storage concerns, weather forecasts, or supply-side constraints in the LNG market—rather than a broad-based demand recovery. Taken together, these signals depict a macro environment where inflation hedges are idle, growth proxies are faltering, and energy costs are being driven by idiosyncratic factors, reinforcing a narrative of stagflationary undertones rather than a clean cyclical upturn or downturn.

The absence of recent China or Hong Kong macro news is itself a signal.
