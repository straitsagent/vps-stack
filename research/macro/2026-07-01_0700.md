### B. Interest Rates & Yield Curve

Critically, the 10-year minus 2-year spread has turned positive at 30 basis points, ending a prolonged inversion that historically preceded recessions; however, this normalization is occurring against a backdrop where the 10-year minus 3-month spread stands at 57 basis points, a more reliable recession indicator that remains firmly positive and thus does not currently trigger the Sahm-adjacent alarm. The fact that both spreads are now positive suggests the economy is in a late-cycle re-steepening phase, often associated with the Fed pausing or cutting, but the absolute level of yields—especially the 10-year above 4.3%—keeps financial conditions restrictive. In credit markets, the divergence between high-yield and investment-grade proxies is telling: HYG is essentially flat near 79.97, indicating that speculative-grade borrowers are not yet under acute stress, while LQD’s 0.6% decline to 109.07 points to duration-driven repricing in higher-quality bonds, as longer-dated paper absorbs the impact of a still-elevated term premium. For equity valuation, this rate environment is a headwind: the 10-year yield above 4.3% raises the discount rate applied to future cash flows, compressing price-to-earnings multiples, particularly for long-duration growth stocks, while the steepening curve benefits value and financial sectors that can earn a positive carry. The overall message is one of cautious positioning: yields are high enough to constrain equity upside, credit spreads are not yet signaling distress, but the steepening curve demands active duration management rather than passive buy-and-hold.

### C. Fed Policy & Inflation

The effective Fed Funds rate sits at 3.63%, a level that, when juxtaposed against the 5-year breakeven inflation rate of 2.26% and the 10-year breakeven of 2.24%, implies a real policy rate of approximately 1.37% to 1.39%. This is a modestly restrictive stance in absolute terms, but it is far from the deeply restrictive territory that would be required to crush demand if inflation were truly entrenched. The critical tension lies in the divergence between these market-based inflation expectations, which remain anchored near the Fed’s 2% target, and the backward-looking hard data. The latest CPI reading of 4.17% year-over-year and PCE of 4.07% represent a stubborn stickiness that is roughly double the breakeven rates, indicating that while markets believe the disinflation trend will continue, the actual price level is still rising at an uncomfortably rapid clip. This gap between realized inflation and expectations is the core of the Fed’s dilemma: they cannot declare victory based on forward-looking indicators alone when the monthly data continues to print well above target.

The labor market adds another layer of complexity. An unemployment rate of 4.30% is a full percentage point above the cycle lows seen in 2023, but it remains historically low and is not yet signaling a recessionary breakdown. This level of unemployment, combined with inflation still above 4%, places the economy in a zone where the traditional Phillips Curve trade-off is not clearly resolved. The Fed is not seeing the kind of labor market deterioration that would force emergency cuts, nor is it seeing the wage-price spiral that would demand aggressive hikes. The Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index, currently at -0.516, is a crucial piece of the puzzle. A negative reading indicates financial conditions that are looser than the historical average, meaning that despite the Fed’s elevated policy rate, equity markets, credit spreads, and lending standards are not tightening in a way that amplifies the central bank’s work. This is a significant headwind for the Fed’s hawkish posture; if financial conditions remain this accommodative, the transmission mechanism of monetary policy is impaired, and the real economy is not feeling the full weight of the 3.63% Fed Funds rate. In stark contrast, Brent crude holding at $76.5 per barrel reflects a market that is balancing tightening supply dynamics—driven by OPEC+ discipline and ongoing geopolitical friction in key producing regions—against a global demand outlook that is resilient but not booming. Conversely, a stabilization or reversal of this trend would signal a reduction in capital flow pressures and a potential catalyst for a re-rating of Chinese assets.
