**Portfolio Weekly Commentary – Week Ending 26 June 2026**

The portfolio declined by 5.98% ($63,173) during the week, closing at $993,470. This marked a significant drawdown driven by a confluence of idiosyncratic shocks in Chinese holdings, a broad rotation out of mega-cap technology, and a sharp divergence between semiconductor capital equipment and software stocks. The portfolio’s heavy concentration in Communication Services (30%) and Technology (29%) amplified the downside, while a 10% allocation to Hong Kong-listed equities proved particularly painful.

**1. Notable Movers and News Catalysts**

The most impactful losses came from the dual-listed Alibaba positions (BABA ADR -24.7%, 9988.HK -18.8%), which together accounted for over $24,000 in losses. The catalyst was unambiguous: Anthropic publicly accused Alibaba of using 25,000 fake accounts to extract 28.8 million conversations from its Claude AI models. This intellectual property theft allegation triggered a sharp re-rating of Alibaba’s AI credibility and raised regulatory and reputational risks. The stock sank to a 16-month low, and the selling was exacerbated by Cathie Wood’s Ark Invest dumping Alibaba shares amid the controversy. Baidu (9888.HK -24.5%) also suffered, likely as contagion from the broader China AI sell-off, despite no direct accusation. The China food delivery sector was further pressured by new regulations on subsidies, dragging Meituan (3690.HK -10.5%) and Trip.com (TCOM -15.6%) lower. Trip.com also faced analyst target cuts from Barclays and Mizuho, reflecting a cautious outlook on Chinese travel demand.

In the US, the Magnificent Seven faced a “magnificent worry” week. Alphabet (GOOGL -9.6%) plunged after the departure of AI genius John Jumper, raising questions about the stability of its AI leadership. Meta (META -8.3%) declined amid broader tech malaise, though news of Zuckerberg exploring prediction market partnerships with Polymarket and Kalshi offered little offset. Amazon (AMZN -5.4%) and NVIDIA (NVDA -7.5%) fell in sympathy with a global technology sell-off, exacerbated by a hotter-than-expected inflation print and geopolitical tensions after President Trump accused Iran of violating a ceasefire. Adobe (ADBE -21.6%) was a standout loser, hitting new 2026 lows. The stock appears to be caught in a rotation away from software toward hardware and AI infrastructure, as CIO surveys from Jefferies showed stronger spending intentions for Microsoft, AWS, and Palo Alto Networks, but not for creative software suites.

**2. Valuation Observations**

Several positions are now trading at significant discounts to analyst targets. Trip.com, for instance, saw its price target slashed from $75 to $60 by Barclays, yet the stock closed the week near $50, implying a 20% upside even to the lowered target. Alibaba’s ADR now trades at roughly 8x forward earnings, a deep discount to its historical average and to US mega-cap peers, reflecting the elevated geopolitical and regulatory risk premium. Adobe, at 21x forward earnings, is near a five-year low multiple, but the market is pricing in structural headwinds from AI competition (e.g., Canva, Midjourney) that may not be fully captured by its still-strong free cash flow margins. Conversely, Applied Materials (AMAT +26.1%) surged to a new high, now trading at over 25x forward earnings, which is elevated relative to its historical average but justified by the AI-driven capex cycle. The divergence between AMAT’s +26% and ADBE’s -21.6% underscores a market that is aggressively rewarding AI hardware exposure while punishing software names perceived as vulnerable to AI disruption.
