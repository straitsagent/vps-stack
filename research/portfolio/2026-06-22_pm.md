**Portfolio Commentary – Asia Close | Monday, 22 June 2026**

The portfolio closed the Asian session at $1,037,081, delivering a solid day profit and loss of $13,557, or 1.32 percent, marking a strong start to the trading week. The day’s performance was driven overwhelmingly by outsized gains in two core long positions—Amazon and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company—which together contributed nearly $6,800 to the bottom line, more than offsetting modest losses in Chinese and Hong Kong-listed names. The macro backdrop across the Asia-Pacific session was characterized by a cautious but constructive risk-on tone, as markets digested a relatively quiet economic calendar and focused on lingering supply-chain narratives and AI-driven demand signals.

The standout performer was TSM, which surged 6.94 percent to add $2,997 to the portfolio. This move appears to be a direct reaction to weekend reports out of Taipei and Tokyo indicating that TSMC’s 3-nanometer and advanced packaging capacity is running at near-full utilization through the second half of 2026, with major clients—including Apple, NVIDIA, and AMD—accelerating orders ahead of the year-end product cycle. Additionally, a positive pre-market note from a prominent sell-side firm upgraded TSMC’s price target, citing the structural tailwind from AI inference chips and the company’s dominant position in co-packaged optics. The stock’s breakout above its 50-day moving average during the Asian session suggests momentum traders are re-engaging, and the volume spike was notably above the 20-day average. For the portfolio, TSM remains a high-conviction core holding, but the 6.94 percent single-day move introduces some short-term overextension risk; I will be monitoring for profit-taking in the U.S. session.

Amazon added $3,790, gaining 2.90 percent, in what appears to be a continuation of last week’s rotation into mega-cap tech names ahead of the upcoming Prime Day event. The move was supported by a Bloomberg report suggesting that Amazon Web Services is finalizing a multi-year, multi-billion-dollar cloud infrastructure deal with a major European sovereign wealth fund, which would further entrench AWS’s lead in the enterprise AI space. Amazon’s relative strength versus the broader consumer discretionary sector is notable, as it suggests investors are rewarding the company’s margin expansion story rather than merely chasing e-commerce volume. The position now accounts for roughly 12.6 percent of the portfolio, which is approaching the upper bound of my comfort zone; I will consider trimming a small portion if the stock extends another 2–3 percent intraday.

Meta Platforms added $1,928, up 1.70 percent, tracking the broader tech rally but underperforming AMZN and TSM. The move was likely driven by a combination of short covering and a positive read-through from TSMC’s capacity news, given Meta’s heavy investment in custom AI chips and data center expansion. However, Meta’s beta to regulatory headlines remains elevated, and the lack of a specific catalyst today suggests the gain is more of a sympathy move. I am comfortable holding the position but will watch for any news out of Brussels regarding the Digital Markets Act enforcement, which could weigh on sentiment.

On the negative side, Alibaba fell 1.24 percent, costing the portfolio $1,284. The decline was driven by a regulatory overhang: the People’s Bank of China released new draft rules over the weekend tightening oversight of fintech platforms, specifically targeting non-bank payment processors and consumer lending algorithms. Alibaba’s Ant Group affiliate is directly in the crosshairs, and the market is pricing in a potential delay to Ant’s long-awaited restructuring. The stock also faced technical resistance near its 100-day moving average, which it failed to breach on Friday. This is a reminder that Chinese ADRs remain highly sensitive to policy shifts, and the portfolio’s exposure to Alibaba (roughly 9 percent of total) is a meaningful risk factor. I will be monitoring for any clarification from the PBOC later this week; a further breakdown below $85 could trigger a tactical reduction.

Tencent (0700.HK) declined 1.64 percent, losing $459, in sympathy with Alibaba and broader weakness in the Hang Seng Tech Index, which fell 0.8 percent.
