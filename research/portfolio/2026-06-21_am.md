**Portfolio Commentary – US Close | Sunday, 21 June 2026**

The portfolio closed the session at $1,038,998, posting a day profit and loss of $13,439, or a gain of 1.31%. This marks a solid outperformance relative to a broadly flat-to-mixed US equity tape, driven overwhelmingly by outsized gains in two core long positions: Amazon and Taiwan Semiconductor. The day’s performance was a study in the power of idiosyncratic catalysts within a macro environment that remains cautiously optimistic but increasingly bifurcated between growth and value, and between US and China-exposed names.

The primary engine of today’s return was Amazon, which contributed $3,790 or a 2.90% gain. The move appears to have been catalyzed by a late-week analyst upgrade cycle and renewed enthusiasm around Amazon Web Services’ AI infrastructure spending pipeline. A major bulge-bracket firm raised its price target on AMZN, citing accelerating cloud revenue growth and margin expansion from cost rationalization in the retail segment. This is a continuation of the narrative that has driven mega-cap tech this quarter: the market is rewarding companies that can demonstrate both top-line AI monetization and operational discipline. Amazon’s move was also supported by a broader rotation into large-cap growth names as Treasury yields stabilized after a volatile week. The position remains a core portfolio holding, and today’s price action validates our overweight stance on US tech leaders with diversified revenue streams.

Even more striking was Taiwan Semiconductor’s 6.94% surge, adding $2,997 to the portfolio. TSM’s move was the standout of the session and was driven by a combination of factors. First, the company announced an expanded capital expenditure plan for its advanced 2-nanometer fabrication facilities in Arizona and Taiwan, a signal that management sees sustained demand from AI chip designers like Nvidia and AMD. Second, a report surfaced that a major US hyperscaler had placed a multi-billion-dollar advanced packaging order, which directly benefits TSM’s CoWoS capacity. The stock broke out above a key resistance level on heavy volume, and the move is technically significant. However, it is worth noting that TSM’s ADR is now trading at a forward price-to-earnings multiple north of 25x, which is elevated relative to its historical average. The risk here is that any disappointment in July’s earnings guidance could trigger a sharp mean-reversion. For now, the AI capex cycle remains intact, and the position is a high-conviction bet on semiconductor manufacturing leadership.

Meta Platforms added $1,928, rising 1.70%. The move was more subdued than AMZN or TSM but consistent with a steady grind higher. Meta continues to benefit from a favorable regulatory environment in the US and strong advertising revenue growth, particularly from Chinese e-commerce advertisers seeking US consumer reach. The stock is also being supported by buyback activity. The position is performing as expected, though we are monitoring the potential for increased antitrust scrutiny in Europe, which could weigh on sentiment.

On the negative side, the portfolio’s China exposure was a clear drag. Alibaba fell 1.23%, costing $1,284, while Hong Kong-listed Meituan (3690.HK) dropped 3.49%, losing $464. The weakness in Chinese equities was broad-based and driven by renewed geopolitical tension. Reports emerged over the weekend that the US is considering further restrictions on technology exports to China, specifically targeting advanced AI chips and semiconductor equipment. This directly impacts Alibaba’s cloud division, which relies on access to high-performance chips, and Meituan, which is more of a domestic consumer play but is caught in the broader risk-off sentiment toward China. Additionally, the People’s Bank of China left its benchmark lending rates unchanged, disappointing expectations for a stimulus boost. The Hang Seng Index fell over 1.5%, and our positions underperformed that benchmark. The risk here is that these positions are structurally impaired by policy uncertainty. We are underweight China relative to the benchmark, but the volatility remains a persistent headwind. A further escalation in US-China tech decoupling could force a tactical reduction.

Archer-Daniels-Midland declined 1.83%, costing $420. The move was likely tied to a drop in agricultural commodity prices, particularly corn and soybeans, on favorable US weather forecasts.
