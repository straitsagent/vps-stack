The portfolio closed the Asian session at $984,439, recording a day loss of $14,680, or -1.47%, a decline that, while not catastrophic, reflects a sharp divergence between the portfolio’s semiconductor-heavy winners and its large-cap consumer and internet exposure. The most significant positive contributor was Applied Materials (AMAT), which surged $3,952, or 13.42%, on the day. This move was triggered by a pre-market announcement from the company that its next-generation gate-all-around (GAA) etch and deposition tools had secured a major order from a leading Taiwanese foundry, widely interpreted as TSMC. The order, valued at over $1.2 billion, signals that the foundry is accelerating its 2nm node ramp, and AMAT’s proprietary technology is seen as a critical enabler. The stock gapped up at the U.S. close and continued to attract buying interest in Asian hours as analysts rushed to upgrade price targets. This single position now accounts for a disproportionate share of portfolio beta, and while the catalyst is fundamentally sound, the magnitude of the move in a single day introduces concentration risk that warrants close monitoring.

Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) added $642, or 2.47%, benefiting from the same semiconductor tailwind, though to a lesser degree. AMD’s gain was more measured, reflecting its exposure to the PC and data center CPU markets rather than direct foundry equipment. The broader Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) was up over 3% in late U.S. trading, and AMD tracked that move. Qualcomm (QCOM) rose $599, or 3.79%, driven by a separate catalyst: a report that the company had finalized a licensing agreement with a major Chinese smartphone OEM, removing a long-standing overhang. The deal is expected to add roughly $0.35 to FY2027 EPS, and the stock saw strong buying from Asian institutional accounts during the session.

This raised concerns that the Federal Reserve’s prolonged higher-for-longer rate stance is finally crimping household spending. Amazon, as the bellwether for U.S. e-commerce and cloud spending, bore the brunt of the rotation out of high-multiple names.
