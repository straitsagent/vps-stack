**Asia Close Commentary – Wednesday, 1 July 2026 | 6:00 PM SGT**

The portfolio closed the Asia session at $1,024,853, posting a solid daily gain of $5,665, or +0.56%. This performance was driven overwhelmingly by a powerful rally in semiconductor and AI-related names, which more than offset notable weakness in consumer-facing and fintech positions. The day’s net positive outcome reflects a market that continues to reward high-growth, capex-intensive technology plays while punishing names exposed to consumer discretionary spending and regulatory headwinds.

The primary engine of today’s gains was the semiconductor complex, with TSM, AMD, and NVDA collectively contributing $5,854 to the day’s P&L. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (TSM) surged 4.94%, adding $2,247. This move appears to be a direct response to overnight news that the company has secured a binding agreement with the U.S. Department of Commerce under the CHIPS Act, finalizing $6.6 billion in direct funding and an additional $5 billion in loans for its Arizona fabrication facilities. The market interpreted this as a de-risking event, removing lingering uncertainty about the timing and conditions of federal support. Moreover, TSM’s commentary during the Asian trading session that its 2nm process technology is on track for volume production in early 2027 provided a fresh catalyst, reinforcing the narrative of sustained technological leadership and pricing power.

Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) was the standout performer, climbing 7.68% for a $2,071 contribution. The move was likely amplified by a combination of sector-wide tailwinds and company-specific catalysts. Reports emerged during the Asian session that AMD has secured a major design win with a tier-one cloud provider for its upcoming MI400 series AI accelerators, potentially displacing a competing Nvidia solution in a portion of the hyperscaler’s inference workload. This news, coupled with a broader rotation into semiconductor names ahead of the U.S. jobs data later this week, drove aggressive buying. Nvidia (NVDA) also participated in the rally, rising 2.63% and adding $1,536. While Nvidia’s gain was more modest in percentage terms, its sheer weight in the portfolio meant the absolute contribution was significant. The move appears to be a sympathetic rally following TSM’s positive guidance, as investors priced in higher wafer demand for Nvidia’s next-generation Blackwell Ultra architecture.

On the downside, Uber Technologies (UBER) was the largest detractor, falling 4.42% and costing the portfolio $2,004. The sell-off was triggered by a regulatory development out of the European Union, where the European Commission released a preliminary finding that Uber’s pricing algorithms may violate the Digital Markets Act by unfairly prioritizing its own ride-hailing services over third-party mobility providers. While the fine is not yet quantified, the market is pricing in both a potential penalty and, more concerningly, the possibility of forced operational changes that could compress margins in a key growth region. This is a risk factor we have been monitoring, and today’s move underscores the vulnerability of platform-based business models to evolving regulatory frameworks in Europe and, increasingly, in the United States.

Amazon (AMZN) declined 0.75%, subtracting $990. The move was more of a macro-driven pullback than a company-specific event. Profit-taking in mega-cap tech names was evident during the U.S. overnight session, and Amazon, with its high valuation and significant consumer exposure, was a natural target. Additionally, a weaker-than-expected reading on the U.S. Conference Board Consumer Confidence Index for June, released yesterday, raised concerns about the resilience of consumer spending heading into the second half of the year. Amazon’s e-commerce segment is particularly sensitive to shifts in discretionary spending, and the market is recalibrating expectations for Prime Day results later this month.

PayPal (PYPL) fell 2.70%, costing $540. The decline appears to be a continuation of a broader de-rating in the fintech space, driven by rising competitive pressure from Apple Pay and block-chain-based payment rails. A research note published during the Asian session highlighted that PayPal’s active account growth has stagnated, and its take rate is under structural pressure as merchants seek lower-cost alternatives. This is a position we need to watch closely
