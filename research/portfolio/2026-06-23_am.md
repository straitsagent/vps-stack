**Portfolio Commentary – US Close | Tuesday, 23 June 2026**

The portfolio closed the session at $1,021,455, suffering a day loss of $17,504, or -1.68%, a decline that was both sharp and concentrated. While the headline loss is uncomfortable, the composition of the day’s moves reveals a clear narrative: a macro-driven rotation out of mega-cap growth and into select semiconductor and industrial names, with our portfolio caught squarely in the crosshairs of a sectoral divergence that punished our largest long positions in Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta while rewarding our smaller but strategically significant bets on Applied Materials, AMD, and TSMC.

The macro backdrop for this session was dominated by a hawkish repricing of Federal Reserve expectations following stronger-than-expected durable goods orders and a surprise uptick in the core PCE services component, which came in at 0.31% month-over-month versus the 0.24% consensus. This data, released during the US morning, effectively dashed hopes for a near-term rate cut and triggered a sharp flattening of the yield curve, with the 2-year Treasury yield rising 8 basis points to 4.72% while the 10-year edged up only 3 basis points to 4.38%. The immediate consequence was a rotation out of long-duration, high-multiple growth stocks—precisely the names that dominate our portfolio’s top holdings. Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta all saw their valuations compressed as the discount rate applied to future cash flows increased, and the market’s risk appetite shifted toward value, cyclicals, and industrials. The Nasdaq Composite fell 1.8% on the day, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average managed a modest gain, underscoring the bifurcation.

The top movers in our portfolio tell the story of this rotation with painful clarity. Amazon fell 4.75%, contributing a $6,380 loss, making it the single largest drag on the day. The move was exacerbated by a bearish analyst note from a major sell-side firm that downgraded AMZN on concerns about slowing AWS revenue growth and margin compression in retail, though the macro rate shock was the primary catalyst. Alphabet’s 4.99% decline, costing us $5,505, was similarly macro-driven, but with an added layer of regulatory overhang after a European Commission statement hinted at new antitrust scrutiny on Google’s digital advertising stack. Meta’s 2.32% drop, while less severe in percentage terms, still cost $2,674, reflecting a broader sell-off in social media and ad-reliant names as the market priced in a higher cost of capital for growth investments in AI infrastructure.

In stark contrast, our semiconductor positions delivered a rare bright spot. Applied Materials surged 3.74%, adding $1,153, driven by a positive industry read-through from a strong earnings report from a key equipment supplier and a bullish update on wafer fabrication equipment spending from TSMC. AMD rose 2.65%, contributing $713, as the stock benefited from a rotation into AI chip plays and a specific catalyst: a leaked roadmap suggesting AMD’s next-generation MI400 accelerator is ahead of schedule, which drove a wave of call buying. TSMC itself gained 1.20%, adding $555, supported by the same capex narrative and a broader Taiwan semiconductor index rally. These gains, however, were insufficient to offset the losses from our mega-cap holdings, as the combined $14,559 loss from AMZN, GOOGL, and META dwarfed the $2,421 gain from the semiconductor trio.

The key position dynamics to note are the concentration risk that this session has laid bare. Our portfolio’s top three holdings—Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta—represent roughly 45% of total assets under management, and all three are highly sensitive to interest rate expectations. When the macro environment shifts against long-duration growth, the portfolio’s beta to that factor is extreme. The semiconductor positions, while welcome diversifiers, are smaller in size and cannot act as a meaningful hedge against a broad growth sell-off. Moreover, the correlation between the mega-cap names and the semis is not stable; today they moved in opposite directions, but historically they often move together on AI sentiment. This session was a reminder that our portfolio is effectively a leveraged bet on the continuation of low rates and strong AI
