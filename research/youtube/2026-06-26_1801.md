**[Will 'Q-Day' save us or harm us? | FT #shorts](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1wqxM3mMDyE)** — Financial Times

The video explains "Q-Day," the anticipated moment when quantum computers become powerful enough to break current cryptographic systems that secure banking, government IDs, and digital currencies. It highlights that quantum computers use qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously, allowing them to solve complex problems much faster than classical computers. While tech giants like Google and IBM aim to have useful quantum machines by 2030, current quantum computers are still prone to engineering errors. The technology offers potential benefits for drug development and fraud protection but also poses serious cybersecurity risks if exploited by bad actors. Cryptocurrency companies are particularly concerned because quantum computing could break the unhackable codes underpinning Bitcoin. The video concludes that while Q-Day could be a false alarm, many companies are already preparing for its arrival.

**[The AI factory: the rewiring of India's tech industry | FT Film](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UfyxN46tCz8)** — Financial Times

Based on the transcript, the FT film explores India's role in the global AI supply chain, focusing on the tension between opportunity and exploitation. The main topic is the "data annotation" industry, where workers in small towns, like Karur, perform repetitive tasks such as wearing cameras to record daily activities, which generates the egocentric data needed to train robots. A key point is that India, while claiming to be the second largest AI workforce, largely acts as a "back office" providing cheap labor for data annotation, a process described as extractive and potentially exploitative. The film highlights that while this work provides income for some, like a new mother earning 1,000 rupees for three hours, it raises concerns about workers being "fodder for robots" and being laid off as AI automates more tasks. A notable conclusion is that the industry concentrates power and value in large tech companies, while Indian workers perform the low-end jobs of training the algorithms that may eventually replace them. The report suggests that although India aims to build its own AI models, its immediate future is tied to a data supply chain that may not create sustainable, high-quality employment.
