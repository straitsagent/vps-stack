**1. Business Overview**

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) operates as the world’s largest dedicated semiconductor foundry, manufacturing, packaging, and testing integrated circuits for fabless customers without designing its own chips. Its core offering includes advanced wafer fabrication processes such as complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) logic, mixed-signal, RF, and embedded memory technologies. Key revenue drivers are high-performance computing and AI accelerators, which accounted for the majority of recent growth, followed by smartphones, automotive, and IoT applications. In 2025, advanced nodes (7 nm and below) represented 74% of wafer revenue, with the 3 nm process alone contributing 28% in Q4.

TSMC’s asset-intensive business model relies on long-term capacity commitments from customers such as Nvidia, Apple, AMD, and Broadcom. The company invests heavily in leading-edge capacity, with monthly 3 nm output expanded to 160,000–175,000 wafers in Q2 2026. Revenue reached NT$416.98 billion in May 2026, up 30.1% year-over-year, driven by sustained AI demand that continues to exceed available supply.

**2. Financial Position**

TSMC’s financial statements show robust top-line expansion. Revenue rose from NT$2,161.74 billion in 2023 to NT$2,894.31 billion in 2024 and NT$3,809.05 billion in 2025. Gross profit and operating income grew in tandem, with net income reaching NT$1,697.60 billion in 2025. Net margins expanded to 45% in 2025 from 39% in 2023, supported by high utilization of advanced nodes. Operating cash flow reached NT$2,274.98 billion in 2025, while free cash flow stood at NT$992.38 billion after NT$1,282.60 billion in capex.

Balance-sheet metrics remain solid. Total debt of NT$1,064.58 billion at end-2025 produced a low debt/equity ratio of 0.2 and a net cash position of NT$1,703.27 billion. The current ratio improved to 2.51. Reported ROE (DuPont) reached 32% in 2025, up from 25% in 2023. Valuation multiples include a trailing P/E of 36.5x, forward P/E of 21.7x, P/B of 7.3x (DB context), and EV/EBITDA of 25.4x. Analyst consensus target stands at $467.84 versus the recent share price near $424.

**3. Competitive Position**

TSMC holds an estimated 70–72% share of the global foundry market, far ahead of peers. Its primary moat stems from physics-driven process leadership, superior yields, and scale that competitors have struggled to match. Advanced-node capacity remains tight despite expansion, allowing TSMC to maintain pricing power and high returns on invested capital.

Key competitors include Samsung Foundry (lagging in yields and market share), Intel (still ramping its foundry ambitions with limited external wins), and smaller players such as United Microelectronics and MediaTek. Peers trade at higher P/E multiples (e.g., MediaTek at 63.3x) despite lower growth and margins. TSMC’s customer base and technology roadmap create high switching costs, reinforcing its position as the default manufacturer for leading-edge AI and HPC chips.

**4. Catalysts**

Near-term upside is supported by persistent AI-driven demand that continues to outstrip supply. TSMC plans 15% price increases on 3 nm wafers in the second half of 2026 to offset rising costs while further tightening capacity allocation. May 2026 revenue growth of 30% year-over-year and guidance for ~30% full-year USD revenue growth provide concrete momentum ahead of the July 16 earnings release.

Additional catalysts include ongoing Arizona fab expansion, which diversifies geographic risk, and continued adoption of 2 nm and beyond processes. Analyst price-target upgrades (BofA to $500, Goldman Sachs higher targets) reflect expectations that AI/HPC will remain the dominant growth driver through at least 2027.

**5. Risks**

Geopolitical tensions surrounding Taiwan represent the most material risk, given the concentration of advanced capacity on the island. Execution risk on overseas fabs and the heavy capex cycle (over NT$1.2 trillion annually) could pressure free-cash-flow conversion if utilization dips. Valuation risk is elevated: even the forward P/E of 21.7x embeds optimistic AI growth assumptions that could disappoint if broader semiconductor demand softens.

Competitive threats from Intel and Samsung remain longer-term but are increasing as both receive government subsidies. Macro headwinds, including potential AI capex digestion or smartphone weakness, could also weigh on near-term order visibility. Short interest is low (0.6%), but any sustained multiple compression would pressure the stock given its premium valuation.
